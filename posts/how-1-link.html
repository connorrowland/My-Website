How A Focus On User Flow And 30 Minutes of Coding Decreased Drop-Off On My Website By 30%

An analysis of what was going wrong with user flow on my personal website, and what I did to fix it.

I like to use my personal website as a place to trying out new things in engineering and design. Sometimes it's using a new font I found—like one I used for headings called Dharma Gothic (a super ~energetic~ typeface). Others could be making a small language practice project, like this iPhone Reproduction I made to brush up on my memory of the DOM and JQuery. This post, however, is about my experience with a user experience/flow project, where I wanted to test out a potential solution to my issue of users dropping off at a mildly alarming rate at different points on my site.

Outline

What distributed drop-off means to me is a poorly defined user flow. I saw that people were coming to my site, selecting certain project pages I had, and then dropping off instead of going through the loop of them. So what I needed to do was create an understandable, easy to follow user loop that would guide people throughout my different project pages, giving users a broad, cohesive view of my work.

What I Did

In order to create this loop I had a few different options. I could just put all of my content on one long page that would continuously scroll until there was no more left to show. But that option felt wrong, as the more content I had the more gigantic this page would become. I also felt this would be disorganized. So I scrapped that idea and decided to focus more on navigation. Specifically, I wanted to place small bits of navigation throughout my portfolio at the end of every portfolio page that would link to the next project in my “loop.” So that’s what I did! And the effects were awesome.

Results

I think the best way to display the results I saw is to define the loop I made, and then show you images of the user flow before and after I pushed up the changes to my site with the end-of-page links.
At this period of time (March 2016) the loop goes as follows (each of these words represents the name of the portfolio file): iPhone, Ellevest, Let's Meet, Trilogy, Baruch, Magazines, and then Pride.

It worked! The loop worked! I was honestly so excited when I saw this—that I could see people going through the exact track/loop I defined. Less people were dropping off on the site as a whole, as well, from 86.7% to 68.3%. And that was just from doing about 30 minutes of coding, which is awesome!

More so than anything, this project taught me about how helpful a user experience can be to the success of a website. I'm happy that I had the opportunity to branch out of my comfort zone and play around with UX, and that finding a personal web "playground" in my portfolio website gave me the place to do that.
